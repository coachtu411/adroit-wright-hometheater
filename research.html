⛔ Client decision update — 2026-07-18 (Tunde, post-call w/ Claudio)

Floor-rising screens are disregarded entirely — do not recommend or reference them going forward. A floor-riser's housing/canister likely requires cutting into the floor, which conflicts with the client's own non-negotiable "no drastic structural or framing changes." This rules out the single-pass research's headline recommendation (Elite Screens Kestrel Tab-Tension 2 CLR3 121" floor-rising, Pairing A below) as a live candidate.

White housing is now a hard requirement (was a soft preference). None of the three new projector candidates (Hisense L9Q, AWOL Vision Aetherion Max, XGIMI Aura) have verified housing color — this must close before any of them can be recommended.

Shortest throw distance is now a hard requirement (was a soft preference). Only the XGIMI Aura's throw (11.7"→120" screen) is verified in this research; the incumbent Optoma ZK420UST's, the Hisense L9Q's, and the AWOL Vision Aetherion Max's throw distances still need direct confirmation before a shortest-throw call can be made.

Net effect: with floor-rising off the table, remaining in-scope screen candidates are the incumbent's own recessed Elite Screens unit (likely non-ALR), the Elite Screens Evanesce recessed line (unpriced), Aeon CLR 5 (fixed frame — sacrifices invisible-when-not-in-use), the Snap One Dragonfly (wall-canister, visible unless boxed into a soffit), and the Draper Access V-Class recessed option paired with BenQ (if upgraded to an ALR surface). A follow-up targeted pass is needed to close housing-color and throw-distance for the remaining projector candidates before any can be recommended over the incumbent.

⚡ Bottom line

Both research passes agree: an ultra-short-throw (UST) laser projector paired with a true ambient-light-rejecting (ALR/CLR) screen is the right category of solution for this daylight-lit room with no blackout curtains — validating the direction the existing $4,861.74 ADI dealer quote (#16708070) was already headed. The Optoma ZK420UST projector in that quote checks out well. But the recessed screen in that same quote is likely a plain, non-ALR surface — possibly the weak link against the room's own ambient-light requirement.

Where they diverge: the adversarially-verified pass says nothing researched is yet proven to beat the incumbent quote — real trade pricing on alternatives is still the missing piece. The single-pass agent went further and named a specific fix (swap to an ALR floor-rising screen). Nothing here has been applied to the live pricing — see the recommendation section below for both takes side by side.

Confirmed UST + ALR/CLR is the established answer for ambient light

High confidence, well-corroborated. The mechanism (diffusion layer over a highly reflective surface + absorptive outer layer) is what lets a UST projector throw a usable image in daylight without blackout curtains. Industry buying guides converge on prioritizing projector brightness headroom and pairing with a UST-specific ALR screen — directly supports the incumbent Optoma ZK420UST + Elite Screens direction as the right category of solution.

UST (Ultra-Short-Throw) projector
A projector designed to sit just inches from the screen — versus several feet for a standard short-throw unit — and still fill it, letting it mount low near a credenza instead of in the ceiling.
ALR / CLR screen (Ambient-Light-Rejecting / Ceiling-Light-Rejecting)
A screen surface engineered to reflect light arriving from a UST projector's low, steep angle while absorbing ambient light coming from overhead room lighting or windows — what lets a projector perform without blackout curtains.
ANSI lumens vs. ISO lumens
Two different projector-brightness measurement standards; figures from each aren't directly comparable. ANSI lumens is the industry's older, more rigorous standard; some newer projectors are rated in ISO lumens instead, which tends to read higher for similar real-world brightness.
Gain
A screen's brightness multiplier versus a flat white reference surface (1.0 gain). Higher gain reflects more light back at the viewer but narrows the usable viewing angle.
Viewing angle
The horizontal range within which the image stays bright and color-accurate. Higher-gain ALR screens typically trade a wider room away for extra brightness.
Throw distance / throw ratio
How far a projector must sit from the screen to fill it at a given size. UST models have a very short throw ratio — a few feet or less — letting them sit near the screen wall instead of at the back of the room.
Trade / dealer pricing vs. retail (MSRP)
Trade pricing is what an AV integrator like Adroit actually pays through a distributor (e.g., ADI, Snap One) — usually well below the publicly listed retail/MSRP price consumers see. This page flags which figure is being cited for every candidate, since only trade pricing is a real cost basis for a client estimate.
Recessed vs. floor-rising vs. fixed-frame screen
Three screen mounting styles: recessed disappears into the ceiling when not in use (most "invisible," most build-out); floor-rising motorizes up from a canister at floor level; fixed-frame stays permanently visible on the wall (cheapest, least flexible).
Laser light source
A projector illumination technology (versus a traditional lamp bulb) rated for a much longer service life — 20,000-30,000+ hours — with more consistent brightness over time and no bulb replacements.
eARC (Enhanced Audio Return Channel)
The HDMI feature carrying high-quality audio from the projector back to the existing Sonos Arc Ultra soundbar over a single cable, avoiding a separate audio run.
Dolby Vision
A dynamic HDR (high-dynamic-range) format that adjusts brightness and color scene-by-scene rather than using one fixed setting for the whole source — noted here as a differentiator on the Hisense PX3-Pro pairing, not a requirement from the original brief.
I'm sourcing a projector + projection screen pairing for a home theater installation for a high-end residential client in Georgetown, Washington DC. Budget is not the primary constraint — picture quality, reliability, and a genuinely premium, invisible-technology aesthetic matter more than shaving cost, but I do need real trade/dealer pricing (not consumer retail) wherever it can be found, since this feeds a contractor's cost basis. Evaluate candidates against the criteria below and do not assume the answer is a projector or screen brand I haven't mentioned — canvas broadly. Non-negotiable constraints: - No blackout curtains — the room has natural daylight and the system must perform well in ambient light. This effectively requires a genuinely bright projector (ANSI lumens, not just peak/dynamic lumens) and/or an ambient-light-rejecting (ALR) screen surface. - No drastic structural or framing changes to the home. - No visible cables, conduit, or hardware — flush, no-baseboard-line aesthetic throughout the home; "super clean, nothing bulky." - No in-wall speakers (audio is handled separately via an existing Sonos system — not in scope for this projector/screen sourcing pass). - Must preserve an existing art-wall accent light — the solution needs to be designed around it, not conflict with its position. Room / physical constraints: - Room: 236" long x 189" wide, ceiling height 117.5". - The wall the screen/projector faces: 117-7/8" wide x 80-1/8" tall, with only 75-1/8" of usable clearance below an existing soundbar mounted on that wall. - Ceiling joist depth in the relevant area is only about 9.25" — tight enough that a fully in-ceiling recessed projector lift is currently marked "needs more engineering info" on suitability. A solution that avoids depending on ceiling joist depth at all is preferable. - Screen size target: 110"-120" diagonal, 16:9. A 135" unit was measured and confirmed too large for the room. - Single-row seating only (no front row). The seated viewing distance is approximately 201" from the back wall to the screen wall. - The screen should be visually invisible when not in use if at all achievable within the above constraints. Preferences (flexible, not hardline): - White or light-colored projector housing preferred. - Built-in smart-TV/streaming platform is a nice-to-have, not required. - Simple, unified control — clean integration via eARC HDMI to an existing Sonos Arc Ultra / Sub 4 / Era 300 surround setup, without adding extra remotes. - Laser (not lamp) light source strongly preferred. Sourcing requirement: - For every candidate, try to identify real AV-trade/dealer pricing (e.g. via ADI Global Distribution) in addition to public retail price, and flag which figure you're citing. Note manufacturer, exact model number, and a source link for every claim. What I need back: 1. A short list (4-6) of projector + screen pairings meeting the non-negotiable constraints, spanning both traditional short-throw ceiling-mount and ultra-short-throw (UST) configurations. 2. For each pairing: model numbers, ANSI lumens, resolution, light source/lifespan, mount type and clearance needed, screen surface/gain and ALR rating, and estimated all-in cost. 3. An explicit call-out of which pairing(s) best solve the ambient-light requirement. 4. Anything a premium client at this price point would reasonably expect that these criteria don't already cover. 5. A final direct recommendation, compared against the two options already on the table.
